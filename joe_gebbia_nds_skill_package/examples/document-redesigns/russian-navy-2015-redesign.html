§ Front · Executive Summary

The summary.

The new technologically advanced Russian Navy, increasingly armed with the KALIBR family of weapons, will be able to more capably defend Russia's maritime approaches and exert significant influence in adjacent seas — a forward-layered defense, a flexible offensive platform, and a stated return to clear great-power status.

Spanpp. iii – iv
Read time~5 min
TypeFraming
§ Exec · 01p. iii

The Russian Navy is in a historic transition — Soviet inventory triaged, modern platforms commissioned, KALIBR-armed.

The publication is intended to provide a basic introduction to the Russian Navy and an appreciation of current developments shaping operations in the 21st century.

Because the U.S. Navy operates worldwide and the Russian Navy is an advanced, globally capable force, its history, capabilities, missions, and role within the Russian state and armed forces should be clearly understood.

"The Russian Navy is being equipped with the newest; including precision long-range strike weapons, and has big nuclear power." — Adm. Viktor Chirkov, CINC Russian Navy
§ Exec · 02p. iii

Over its 320-year history, the navy has periodically battled to maintain access to the world's seas.

The navy has supported land forces in times of imperial expansion and in the expulsion of invaders, been part of the strategic nuclear deterrence triad, and demonstrated a global presence in the national interest. Political and military strategy and the role of the navy evolved as the Russian state grew, faced challenges, was transformed into the Soviet Union by revolution, repelled invasion, and encountered disintegration.

Throughout, the navy retained many of the basic organizational, procedural, and personnel practices laid down by its founder, Peter I (the Great), at the beginning of the 18th century. As the years passed, naval activity and shipbuilding capabilities required to advance it became ever more sophisticated and varied.

§ Exec · 03p. iv

Today's Russian Navy is roughly one-sixth to one-quarter the size of the Soviet Navy at its peak — and is more capable per platform.

As Russia asserts itself on the world stage, it is giving priority of effort and funding to recapitalizing its navy. Major transition is underway from the legacy Soviet Navy to a Russian Navy that should reflect the latest achievements of Russian advances in science and technology.

On the basis of currently available data it is projected that the Russian Navy will retain its core missions. Although the national defense mission of the strategic and general purpose navy has remained, today's fiscal realities require that the decreased number of major platforms be multi-mission capable and armed with the latest capabilities in weapons; sensors; and C4ISR systems.

Fleet disposition · the four-and-one-half navies.

FIG. 01 · Geographic disposition · 2015 baseline

Geography forces a particular structure on the Russian Navy. The coastline is more than 2.5 times the length of the land frontier and is fragmented across four separated maritime theaters plus the Caspian. Each fleet must be effectively self-sufficient to perform its missions in its region of responsibility — the strategic utility of sending ships from one fleet to support another in defensive missions is constrained by transit distance.

The Northern and Pacific Fleets host the strategic nuclear-powered ballistic missile submarine (SSBN) force. The Baltic and Black Sea Fleets are coastal-defense and Mediterranean-approach forces. The Caspian Flotilla is a small but operationally significant patrol and missile-armed force — demonstrated by KALIBR strikes into Syria from the Caspian during the Russian intervention.

§ 01 · Strategy

National missions.

Over three centuries, Russian naval strategy has transitioned from developing approaches to acquire access to open seas in the west, to developing effective and credible anti-access approaches and capabilities to deter or defend against long-range aerospace capabilities. Today, the need for access to open seas remains a vital concern in the Baltic and Black Seas.

§ 1.2 · DIAGRAM

Layered defense.
Cross-section seen from above.

The Russian Navy's defensive thesis is that potential enemy forces should be engaged as soon as their long-range weapons can threaten and be brought to bear on Russian territory. The notional outer ring is the TOMAHAWK Land Attack Missile range — about 1,000 nautical miles from the Russian frontier.

The diagram at right inverts the source's flat-map presentation into a cross-section, showing the three concentric zones the doctrine names: forward defense (the outer ring, deny long-range strike), intermediate / close-in defense (smaller combatants, diesel submarines, coastal defense), and the strategic bastions — protected SSBN patrol areas in adjacent seas.

§ 2.1p. 7

The Russian Navy is composed of interconnected components, from combat forces to supporting infrastructure.

Command staff, submarine forces, surface forces, naval auxiliaries, naval aviation, naval infantry, coastal missile and artillery troops, and the naval shore establishment — including headquarters, communications, intelligence, maintenance and repair, education and training.

The headquarters of the Russian Navy was recently relocated from Moscow to its historical home in the Admiralty building in St. Petersburg. The new Navy Command Center is functioning. The CINC officially runs the Navy from the new headquarters, but will always share time between the seat of government in Moscow and St. Petersburg.

§ 2.2p. 9

Admiral Viktor Chirkov, CINC since May 2012 — a Pacific-Baltic surface officer.

Chirkov is a lifelong surface officer with extensive experience in the Pacific and Baltic Fleets. He rose through commander of an UDALOY I destroyer, group command, combined force command of all Russian forces on Kamchatka, then Chief of Staff and Commander of the Baltic Fleet.

Upon his appointment, Chirkov said: "The most important thing for Russia is to build the fleet with support of the president and like-minded persons. My experience of commanding the fleet on the country's western maritime frontier will allow me to assess the full importance and responsibility of this appointment."

"Thanks to the bravery of sailors, the talent of shipbuilders, and the daring of explorers, pioneers, and naval leaders, our nation has held fast as a great maritime power." — Vladimir Putin · Russian Federation Navy Day · 26 July 2015
§ 2.3p. 11

Future leadership · geographically stationary career paths.

Due to the enormity of the Russian land, and the difficulty and expense of moving and finding suitable housing, most of the Russian officer corps tends to remain relatively geographically stationary. Service schools — five naval commissioning schools — are the predominant path. Most officers attend the one nearest their home.

The St. Petersburg Naval Institute Peter the Great Corps (established 1701) is the premier school. Promotions through Captain 3rd Rank (O-4) are dominantly within the immediate commanding officer's purview. Higher ranks involve decisions by formation and fleet commanders. Flag ranks are the combined purview of Navy Command and the Ministry of Defense.

The classic path to senior command runs through the navigation specialty — with weapons as a close second. The system trains "masters of their craft," not generalists.

§ 03 · Procurement

Quality over quantity.

The 1995–2005 fallow years were used to develop new, more modern, and capable designs. The organization and fiscal stability achieved since 2005 has permitted budgeting for and financing of critically needed new construction. The Russian Navy still retains the essential missions of its Soviet predecessor — accomplished with fewer means and at lesser cost.

§ 3.1p. 15

The financial recovery enabled what the 1990s could not — new construction at scale.

Today's Russian Navy is about one-sixth to one-quarter the size of the Soviet Navy at its heyday. The fallow years were used to develop new, more modern, and capable designs and systems. Since 2005, fiscal stability has permitted budgeting for critically needed new construction.

The Navy retains the essential missions of its Soviet predecessor, but with the recognition that today's global realities may allow accomplishment with fewer means and at lesser cost — advantageous in an era when each generation of armaments takes more than a decade to develop, design, and build.

§ 3.2p. 15

Quality, defined: multi-mission platforms with KALIBR-class precision strike.

The shift to quality means each major platform carries the latest weapons, sensors, and C4ISR. The KALIBR family of cruise missiles — surface-launched, submarine-launched, demonstrated operationally in 2015 Syria strikes from the Caspian Flotilla — is the headline capability.

Multi-mission capability is the operative requirement: a smaller surface combatant must contribute to anti-surface, anti-submarine, anti-air, and land-attack missions to justify the unit cost. The Soviet practice of single-purpose hulls — minesweepers, ASW corvettes, missile boats — is giving way to general-purpose combatants.

§ 3.3p. 17

The future fleet · SSBNs first, surface combatants second, aviation last.

SSBN recapitalization leads: the Borei-class SSBN (Yuriy Dolgorukiy and successors) replaces aging Delta-IIIs and Delta-IVs. Submarine forces are the priority of priorities — the bastion strategy depends on them.

Surface modernization includes new frigates (Admiral Gorshkov class), corvettes (Steregushchy class), and patrol craft. The carrier program — a single Kuznetsov-class CV with limited operational availability — is more aspirational than operational and remains a long-cycle project.

Naval aviation, including long-range maritime patrol (Tu-142 Bear-F) and shipborne fighter (Su-33 / MiG-29K), is the slowest-moving leg of the recapitalization.

§ 04 · Personnel · Outlook

Toward a professional force.

The trend is from a conscript navy toward a contract-professional one — slower than Russian leadership hoped, faster than Soviet legacy would predict. The post-2005 stability allows it. The 2014 Crimea operation and 2015 Syria intervention put it under operational test.
