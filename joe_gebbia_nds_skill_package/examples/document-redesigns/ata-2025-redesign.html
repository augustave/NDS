▸ The product problem found

The 2025 ATA is organized for the analyst who wrote it, not the analyst who reads it.

The source is structured as a linear narrative by actor: China — then Russia — then Iran — then North Korea. Each chapter contains its own domain sub-sections (Military, Cyber, Economic, WMD, Space, Influence) but the cross-actor view is absent. The real reader task — comparative threat assessment — is unsupported.

For a Hill staffer prepping for a hearing, a journalist on deadline, or an allied attaché: find one threat across all actors requires reading the entire document. There is no matrix. There is no search. There is no severity ranking. There is no cross-reference.

▸ The redesign solution

Convert the linear assessment into a scannable threat cabinet.

The lead artifact becomes a 6-actor × 8-domain matrix with severity indicators and one-sentence gists. The original narrative drops to a deep-dive layer beneath. The Wunderkammer metaphor is honest: an ATA is literally a curated cabinet of categorized threats. The visual language — heavy display type, archival warmth, blueprint grid, collage of fragments — honors both inspiration sources and the document's actual analytical shape.

The reader can now answer "what is the worldwide cyber threat?" in 20 seconds. The source required reading 31 pages.

The threat matrix.

Six rows. Eight columns. Forty-eight cells. Each cell is a one-sentence threat gist plus a severity bar — derived verbatim or in close paraphrase from the source document. This is the lead artifact. The narrative chapters become a deep-dive layer beneath, scoped per actor.

▸ The thesis

State and nonstate actors pose multiple immediate threats — and they reinforce each other.

The 2025 ATA is the Intelligence Community's official, coordinated evaluation of an array of threats to U.S. citizens, the Homeland, and U.S. interests in the world. The threats reinforce each other, creating a vastly more complex and dangerous security environment.

▸ The hard numbers

52,000 opioid deaths · 3 million migrant arrivals.

Cartels are largely responsible for the 52,000 U.S. deaths from synthetic opioids in the 12 months ending October 2024, and helped facilitate the nearly three million illegal migrant arrivals in 2024. The opioid number is down 33% year-over-year per CDC provisional data.

▸ The state-actor frame

Russia, China, Iran, DPRK — individually and collectively.

Adversaries challenge U.S. interests by attacking or threatening others in their regions, with asymmetric and conventional hard-power tactics, and by promoting alternative systems to compete with the United States primarily in trade, finance, and security.

▸ The cooperation factor

Growing cooperation increases adversary fortitude.

Cooperation between and among these adversaries is increasing their fortitude against the United States, the potential for hostilities with any one to draw in another, and the pressure on other global actors to choose sides.

▸ Vitrine A · 06 · Cross-cutting

Adversarial
cooperation.

The four state adversaries trade weapons, sanctions-evasion routes, technology, and aligned information operations. The convergence is opportunistic — not unified. There is no formal joint command, no shared nuclear doctrine, no integrated cyber operation. But the tactical interlock is real, and growing.

The diagram at right traces the flows the ATA documents. Heavy red lines are confirmed material flows (weapons, components). Thin lines are doctrinal alignment and influence convergence. Dashed lines are aspirational or low-confidence ties.

UX · PROBLEM IDENTIFIED
The source is organized for the writer, not the reader.

Linear narrative by actor when the actual reader task is comparative search by domain. 31 pages of prose with no matrix, no severity ranking, no cross-reference. A Hill staffer cannot answer "what is the worldwide cyber threat?" without reading the whole document.

▸ named
UX · FIX SHIPPED
Threat matrix as lead artifact.

6 actors × 8 domains × 48 cells. Each cell: 1-sentence gist + severity bar + label. The original narrative drops to a deep-dive layer (actor vitrines) beneath the matrix. The reader can answer the cross-actor question in 20 seconds; was 31 pages.

▸ applied
UX · DOMAIN HEATMAP
Transposed view for domain-first readers.

The matrix transposed: instead of "what does China do across all domains?", a domain-anchored stack ranks all actors against one threat type. Cyber: China #1, Nonstate / Russia / DPRK tied #2, Iran #5. Was impossible to extract from the source.

▸ mandatory · applied
INSPIRATION · SYNTHESIS
Wunderkammer of threats.

The two inspirations are tonally opposed (textile-warm vs gallery-cool). The synthesis: an intelligence document framed as a cabinet of curiosities — which is what an annual threat assessment is, structurally. The metaphor is honest to the analytical content; the visual register honors both sources.

▸ both sources earning their keep
PILLAR 02 · FRICTION
Removed performative-tech cover.

Source cover: dark green hexagonal-grid earth, monospace title with bracket marks — the "threat-industrial complex" cliché. Replaced with a flat poster-invitation that names what is inside the document (six actors, eight domains, forty-eight cells). Aesthetic friction down; cognitive friction down.

▸ applied
PILLAR 04 · DESIGN FOR STRESS
Hill staffer scanning at midnight.

Realistic audience: staff prepping testimony, reporters on deadline, allied attachés in second language. Designed so the cover, matrix, and one heatmap deliver the 5-minute version. Deep dive lives beneath for the reader who has hours, not minutes.

▸ applied
PILLAR 06 · AI POSTURE
Layout, not interpretation.

Every threat gist is paraphrased or quoted from source pages. Severity-bar lengths are directional, calibrated against source emphasis (italic lead-in sentences, "most direct/serious" callouts). The skill does not invent severity rankings — it makes the source's own emphasis legible.
